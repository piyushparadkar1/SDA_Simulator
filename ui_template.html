Section 1: Process Overview

Solvent Deasphalting (SDA) is a solvent extraction process used in petroleum refining to separate heavy vacuum residue (VR) into two fractions: Deasphalted Oil (DAO) and asphalt (also called pitch or bitumen). The process exploits differences in solubility of hydrocarbon classes in light paraffinic solvents at near-supercritical conditions.

The VR feed — the heaviest fraction from atmospheric and vacuum distillation — contains saturates, aromatics, resins, and asphaltenes (SARA classes). At elevated pressure and moderate temperature, a light alkane solvent (propane or butane) selectively dissolves the lighter, more paraffinic components (saturates and some aromatics) while rejecting the heavy asphaltenic and resinous components.

The Supercritical Extraction process recovers solvent by operating near its critical point, where the solvent density drops sharply with a small temperature increase. This enables energy-efficient solvent recovery without vaporisation — the DAO-rich phase separates as a dense liquid that is then flashed to recover propane.

DAO products are used as: (a) Lube bright stock base oil — a high-viscosity index lubricant base requiring very low CCR and colour, (b) FCC feed — a catalytic cracker feedstock tolerating higher contamination in exchange for higher yield. The trade-off between yield and quality is the core operating challenge.

Asphalt product is used for: road paving bitumen (if metals content is acceptable), fuel oil blending, or gasification feed. Its value is significantly lower than DAO.

The 3-bed packed column extractor operates countercurrently: VR feed enters below the middle bed, fresh solvent enters at the bottom, and the DAO-rich extract exits from the top. LP steam coils between the middle and top bed create a temperature gradient that acts as internal reflux, improving selectivity by weakening the solvent near the top (higher temperature = lower propane density = weaker solvent power).

The three beds serve distinct functions: the bottom bed handles asphalt rejection in a strong-solvent zone (fresh propane only, no feed yet), the middle bed is the primary extraction zone where feed is introduced, and the top bed is the polishing zone that determines final DAO colour and CCR.

Section 2: Model Description

Feed Representation. The vacuum residue is represented as a continuous mixture of pseudo-components, each characterised by molecular weight, SARA class, density, and solubility parameter. The SARA fractions (Saturates, Aromatics, Resins, Asphaltenes) are estimated from feed density, CCR, and viscosity using empirical correlations calibrated to heavy VR data (Speight 2020, Lian et al. 2014). Each SARA class spans a MW distribution (Gauss-Legendre quadrature, typically 5 components per class). This approach captures the polydisperse nature of VR in a computationally tractable way.

K-Value Model (default, fast). Equilibrium partition coefficients Ki = wiDAO / wiasphalt are computed from: (1) SARA-class base K from molecular weight using calibrated power-law parameters, (2) solubility parameter spread modifier (delta_crit) that shifts resin/asphaltene partitioning, (3) density-based solvent power correction: K ×= (ρpropane/ρref)α where ρref = 0.44 g/cm³ and α = 3.0. This density correction is the key physical insight: propane density decreases with rising temperature at constant pressure, reducing K-values and DAO yield. It simultaneously captures both temperature and pressure effects correctly.

PHCT Model (rigorous, slower). The Perturbed Hard Chain Theory EOS computes fugacity coefficients φi for each component in each phase. K-values are updated iteratively from Kinew = φiII/φiI using successive substitution with damping for stability. The K-value model provides the initial guess. PHCT is thermodynamically self-consistent and accounts for associative interactions (resins, asphaltenes have higher polarity parameters), but is ~10× slower per stage.

3-Bed Countercurrent Extractor. The stage-wise method solves the countercurrent cascade with 4 theoretical stages (Bottom: 2, Middle: 1, Top: 1 at HETP=2000mm). Each stage performs a Rachford-Rice flash (phase split calculation) followed by asphaltene precipitation kinetics and Murphree stage efficiency correction. Predilution propane injection is modelled as a reduced effective S/O ratio in the lower stages.

Key Assumptions and Limitations. (1) Equilibrium is assumed at each theoretical stage — real stages may have lower efficiency (adjust E_murphree). (2) The K-value correlation is calibrated to propane at 35–45 bar; extrapolation outside this range may be unreliable. (3) Metals (Ni, V) partitioning is not modelled — the simulator does not predict DAO metals content. (4) Temperature profiles are estimated from inlet conditions using an energy balance; actual profiles depend on heat exchanger performance. (5) The model is most accurate near its calibration point.

Why density (not temperature) drives extraction physics. At constant pressure, increasing temperature decreases propane density from ~0.52 g/cm³ at 60°C to ~0.35 g/cm³ at 85°C (at 40 bar). This is the dominant effect on solvent power. The density correction (ρ/ρref)3.0 captures both the strong temperature dependence and the weaker pressure dependence in a single physically-grounded correlation.

V2.0 Addition: PINN Hybrid Digital Twin Layer

Why a hybrid model? The physics engine is accurate near its calibration point but cannot capture all plant-specific deviations: crude blend shifts, column internals degradation, seasonal propane quality changes, and systematic sensor offsets. A Physics-Informed Neural Network (PINN) layer learns the residual discrepancy between physics predictions and plant measurements without replacing the thermodynamic foundation.

Discrepancy learning principle. The PINN corrects multiplicatively: corrected = physics × (1 + δ) where δ is a small learned correction. This preserves all physical constraints (mass balance, monotonicity with S/O) while allowing data-driven adaptation to plant reality. Corrections are clamped: |δvisc| ≤ 0.5, |δyield| ≤ 0.3.

Operating regime awareness. The plant operates in distinct regimes (lube-grade low S/O, FCC-grade high S/O, seasonal crude shifts). A PCA + Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) clusters DCS telemetry into 2–5 operating modes (number chosen automatically via BIC). The active cluster is passed as a one-hot input to the PINN, giving it regime-specific correction capability.

Network architecture. Two tiny MLP networks share one feature normaliser: (1) Viscosity net: 10 inputs → 8 neurons (Tanh) → 1 output ≈ 89 parameters. (2) Yield net: 10 inputs → 16 neurons (Tanh) → 1 output ≈ 177 parameters. Networks are deliberately tiny because only ~10–14 LIMS viscosity measurements per year are available — overfitting prevention dominates the design.

Physics-informed composite loss: Ltotal = Ldata,visc + Ldata,yield + λmb·Lmass_balance + λmono·Lmonotonicity + λL2·Lweight_decay. The mass balance penalty penalises yields outside [0%, 100%]. The monotonicity penalty enforces that higher S/O always produces equal or higher corrected yield. L2 weight decay prevents sparse LIMS over-fitting. Default λmb=10, λmono=1, λL2=0.01.

Graceful degradation. If PyTorch is missing, if training fails, or if PINN test metrics are more than 10% worse than OLS on either metric, the PINN is silently discarded and OLS corrections remain in effect. The active correction mode ('ols' or 'pinn') is always reported in calibration results.

Inputs used by the button:

What is ΔT Steam?

ΔTsteam is the temperature rise added by the LP steam heating coil in the top bed. It is the difference between Ttop and Tmiddle.

Formula:

Q_steam  = steam_flow [kg/hr] × h_fg [kJ/kg] / 3600          → kW
m_fluid  = 0.80 × (feed + solvent) [kg/hr] / 3600            → kg/s
ΔT_steam = (Q_steam × effectiveness) / (m_fluid × Cp)    → °C

Worked example (design conditions: feed=90°C, propane=54°C, steam=2830 kg/hr, P=4.5 kg/cm²g):

T_bottom = 54 + 0.35 × (90 − 54)                 = 66.6 °C
T_middle = 66.6 + 0.55 × (90 − 66.6)             = 79.5 °C

Steam at 4.5 kg/cm²g:  P_abs = 5.42 bar,  h_fg = 2083 kJ/kg
Q_steam  = 2830 × 2083 / 3600                        = 1638 kW
m_fluid  = 0.80 × (45000 + 360000) / 3600            = 90.0 kg/s
ΔT_steam = (1638 × 0.60) / (90.0 × 2.3)              = 4.8 °C  (capped at 20°C)

T_top    = 79.5 + 4.8                                  = 84.3 °C

Key insight about steam pressure: Higher pressure LP steam has lower latent heat hfg (because it is closer to saturation). So a drop in LP steam header pressure (e.g. 4.5 → 2.0 kg/cm²g) actually increases hfg from 2083 → 2139 kJ/kg and thus slightly increases ΔTsteam for the same flow. This is counter-intuitive — monitor steam pressure on the DCS header PI.

Flash warning: If Ttop is within 3°C of the propane saturation temperature (Tsat ≈ 87°C at 40 kg/cm²g), a red warning is displayed. Operating above Tsat risks propane vapour lock in the top bed, causing flooding and yield collapse.

Section 4: Outputs and Predictions

DAO Yield [wt%]. Mass fraction of VR that reports to the DAO product, expressed as wt% of VR feed. Typical range: 15–35 wt% for propane solvent, 40–60 wt% for butane. Lube Bright Stock mode targets ~18 wt%, FCC Feed mode targets ~28–35 wt%. The simulator predicts the gross yield before any solvent entrainment corrections. Mass balance: DAO yield + Asphalt yield ≈ 100 wt% (small deviations from stage iteration convergence).

DAO Viscosity [cSt @100°C]. Estimated from a correlation based on DAO average molecular weight, density, and SARA composition. The correlation is: visc = exp(a × ln(MW) + b/T + c × resin_fraction + d) where coefficients are calibrated to DAO data. Limitations: (1) Only valid for propane DAO — butane DAO has different characteristics, (2) actual plant viscosity depends on precise cut point and blending, (3) uncertainty ±20–30% is typical for this type of correlation.

ASTM D1500 Colour. Predicted from asphaltene contamination level in the DAO and the resin fraction in SARA_DAO. Scale is 0 (water-white) to 8 (dark brown). Typical targets: lube DAO <3.0, FCC DAO <6.0. The colour is very sensitive to trace asphaltene carry-over. Colour increases sharply above an asphaltene contamination threshold (~0.5 wt% in SARA_DAO). Prediction uncertainty is high (±1–2 ASTM units).

Asphaltene Contamination [wt%]. Mass fraction of asphaltene-class components (by SARA classification) in the DAO product. This is the primary quality indicator for colour and CCR. Values <0.1 wt% are excellent, 0.1–0.5 wt% is good for FCC, >0.5 wt% indicates colour issues. Note: this is NOT the same as measured pentane-insolubles, which are typically lower.

Hydraulic Warnings. The simulator checks superficial velocity in each bed against limits for the structured packing: warning at 0.015 m/s, near-flooding at 0.020 m/s. The bottom bed (ID=3000mm) is the bottleneck due to its smaller diameter. Phase density difference below 0.10 g/cm³ indicates risk of phase inversion. A propane saturation warning is issued if the top-bed temperature approaches the propane bubble point (T_sat ≈ 87°C at 40 bar).

Prediction Limitations. (1) All quality predictions (viscosity, colour, CCR) are correlations — not first-principles calculations. Calibration with plant data significantly improves accuracy. (2) The model does not predict DAO metals content (Ni, V). (3) Predilution effects on viscosity and pour point are not fully captured. (4) The model is isothermal at each stage — actual temperature profiles have radial gradients not captured here.

Data quality notes: Rows where both trains are offline (feed_flow < 2 m³/hr) are dropped. Single-train operation is preserved. Feed density and CCR are forward-filled from sparse LIMS measurements (defaults: 1.028 g/cm³, 22.8 wt%). DAO viscosity is never forward-filled — each measurement is an independent calibration point. A 2-hour rolling steady-state filter (temperature std < 1.5°C) removes transient operation. LIMS viscosity reflects extractor conditions 3 hours before the sample (LIMS lag correction applied). Train split is chronological 80/20 — the most recent 20% is held out as a test set and is never used during optimisation.

Phase 0: Thermal Calibration

Fits a linear temperature-profile model to predict T_bottom, T_middle, T_top from DCS inlet tags (feed temp, propane temp, steam flow, steam pressure). Runs on all steady-state DCS rows. This phase decouples the temperature uncertainty from the extraction physics calibration in subsequent phases. Calibrated T-profile coefficients are fixed for Phases 1–4.

Phase 1: OLS Viscosity Correction

Fits two parameters: visc_slope and visc_bias applied as a post-correction after the physics engine: corrected_visc = visc_slope × physics_visc + visc_bias. Only LIMS-anchored rows (where a measured dao_visc_100 exists) are used. Viscosity residuals are computed on a log scale to avoid bias toward high-value outliers. A fallback to bias-only correction activates if the OLS correlation coefficient is below 0.15 (indicating the physics model viscosity trend does not match plant trend).

Phase 2: K_multiplier Optimiser

Minimises a weighted residual objective over all training rows using scipy least_squares (Trust Region Reflective method). The single primary free parameter is:

  • K_multiplier [0.70–1.40]: Scale factor on all K-values. Shifts DAO yield up or down while preserving the shape of the extraction curve. Re-anchored at K_ref=0.6448 corresponding to ~15.9 vol% DAO at design conditions (S/O=9, T_top=82°C).
  • DAO yield weight = 0.4, viscosity weight = 1.0 in the combined objective. Viscosity receives higher weight because yield is calculated from mass balance and is typically more reliable than model yield.

Optional Path B extends the optimisation to also calibrate c_t_sat (C_T solubility correction): K ×= exp(c_t × (1/T − 1/T_ref)). c_t_aro = 0.70 × c_t_sat; resins and asphaltenes use fixed C_T = 0.

When to Recalibrate

  • Feed crude blend changes significantly (new VR density or CCR >±3%)
  • Sustained yield bias > 3 wt% after stabilisation following a crude switch

Section 6: PINN & Hybrid Digital Twin Mode

What is PINN Hybrid mode? When you check Enable PINN Hybrid Mode before running calibration, the pipeline adds Phases 3 and 4 after the standard OLS calibration. A Physics-Informed Neural Network (PINN) learns the systematic discrepancy between the physics engine predictions and actual plant measurements, capturing non-linear regime-dependent errors that the linear OLS correction cannot.

Phase 3: Operating Regime Detection

The plant operates in distinct regimes that affect extraction behaviour beyond what the single-point physics model captures. Examples: lube-grade operation (tight quality, S/O 6–8), FCC-grade operation (yield maximisation, S/O 9–12), winter crude (high CCR, low viscosity feed), summer crude (lower CCR). A PCA + Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) automatically identifies these regimes from the DCS telemetry.

How regime detection works:

  1. Feature set: so_ratio (trains A & B), t_top, t_bot, feed_flow, propane_temp — 9 DCS tags per hour.
  2. StandardScaler: normalises each feature to zero mean, unit variance. This prevents high-flow tags from dominating low-temperature tags.
  3. PCA: reduces 9 dimensions to the fewest components that explain 95% of variance (typically 4–5 components). This removes correlated noise between tags.
  4. GMM with BIC selection: fits Gaussian Mixture Models for 2, 3, 4, and 5 clusters; selects the number that minimises the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). BIC penalises model complexity, preventing over-clustering on noisy data.
  5. Cluster ID output: each hourly DCS row gets a soft probability over all clusters plus a hard cluster assignment. The active cluster ID is one-hot encoded as an additional input to the PINN.

Why PCA+GMM instead of SDAE+FCM? The 9 DCS features are mostly linearly correlated (temperature tags co-move with S/O ratio). PCA captures 95% of variance in 4 components trivially. GMM provides the same soft memberships as FCM but with proper model selection via BIC (no manual cluster count needed). SDAE and FCM add training complexity and hyperparameter tuning for no measurable gain on this dataset size.

Phase 4: PINN Training

Forward pass cache. Before training, the calibrated physics engine (with K_mult from Phase 2) is run on all training rows in parallel (joblib multiprocessing). This produces (physics_visc, physics_yield) for each row. The cache is computed once — the physics engine is never called during PINN gradient computation, keeping training fast.

PINN targets: δvisc = (measured_visc / physics_visc) − 1 and δyield = (measured_yield / physics_yield) − 1. The networks learn these fractional corrections, then inference applies: corrected = physics × (1 + δ).

Physics-informed loss function:

Loss term Weight (λ) Physics constraint enforced
Ldata,visc1.0MSE on log-scale viscosity (LIMS-anchored rows only)
Ldata,yield1.0MSE normalised by yield standard deviation (all steady-state rows)
Lmass_balance10.0Penalises corrected yield outside [0%, 100%] and |δ| exceeding clamp bounds

Lweight_decay0.01L2 regularisation — prevents memorisation of the sparse LIMS viscosity points

Training uses Adam optimiser (lr=0.001, ReduceLROnPlateau scheduler), early stopping with patience=20 epochs on the chronological validation split (most recent 20% of training data). Maximum 200 epochs; typical convergence at 50–100 epochs.

Quality Gate & Deployment Decision

After training, the PINN is evaluated on the held-out chronological test set (most recent 20% of the full dataset). The quality gate compares PINN test MAE against OLS test MAE for both viscosity and yield:

  • PINN deployed (correction_mode = 'pinn'): PINN MAE ≤ OLS MAE × 1.10 for both metrics. The PINN improves or at least does not degrade test performance by more than 10%.
  • PINN discarded (correction_mode = 'ols'): PINN MAE > OLS MAE × 1.10 on either metric. OLS corrections from Phase 1–2 remain in effect. This result is shown with a warning in the PINN status panel.

The quality gate exists because with only ~10–14 LIMS viscosity points per year, even a tiny network can show apparent training improvement while over-fitting to the training split. The chronological test set is the definitive out-of-sample check. If the gate fails, it means the current training data is insufficient for PINN generalisation — collect more LIMS measurements or wait for a seasonal crude change that adds a new operating point.

Graceful Degradation Hierarchy

  1. PyTorch not installed: PINN checkbox is ignored; calibration completes with OLS only. No error shown to user; correction_mode = 'ols' in output JSON.
  2. Fewer than 5 LIMS visc rows: PINN training skipped (insufficient data for chronological validation split); OLS used.
  3. PINN training exception: Error is logged to server console; OLS correction is preserved; result JSON includes pinn_result.error key.
  4. Quality gate failure: PINN trained successfully but test metrics worse; PINN checkpoints saved to disk for inspection but OLS applied at inference.
  5. PINN inference exception at runtime: simulator_bridge.py catches the exception, sets correction_mode = 'ols_fallback', and returns the OLS-corrected result unchanged.

Underlying Design Principles

  • Physics-first: The frozen physics engine (Hunter-Nash extractor, PHCT EOS, Murphree efficiency) is never modified by training. The PINN only adds a bounded multiplicative correction on top. This ensures all fundamental thermodynamic constraints are always respected.
  • Sparse-data regularisation: Every architectural choice (89-param visc net, physics loss penalties, L2 decay, early stopping, quality gate) exists to prevent overfitting to the sparse LIMS dataset. Adding more parameters to the PINN would not improve accuracy — it would increase overfitting risk.
  • Chronological split invariant: The 80/20 train/test split is always chronological, never random. Shuffling a time-series produces artificially optimistic validation metrics because training points adjacent in time to test points leak correlated information. The chronological split measures true out-of-sample generalisation.
  • Interpretable corrections: The correction magnitudes δvisc and δyield are bounded and reported in calibration output. An unexpectedly large correction (|δ| > 0.3) is a diagnostic signal that the physics model needs re-anchoring, not just a PINN correction.
  • Reproducible deployment: PINN checkpoints (weights + normaliser + regime detector) are saved to calibration_profiles/ alongside the OLS profile JSON. The same checkpoint is loaded at every inference call, giving deterministic predictions regardless of runtime state.
